Q1. Induction Motor Analysis

Topic Introduction: Induction motors operate on the principle of electromagnetic induction. The relationship between the torque (turning force) and the slip (speed loss) defines the efficiency and starting capability of the machine.

Consider the statements:
1. Max torque is independent of rotor resistance.
2. Max torque is proportional to square of applied voltage.
3. At starting, slip is zero.
4. Increasing rotor resistance shifts max torque towards higher slip.

Analyzes the relationship between voltage and torque but ignores the slip-shift property of resistance.
Includes the independence of T-max from R2 and the quadratic relationship with voltage.
Focuses on starting conditions but misrepresents the numerical value of slip at standstill.
Combines mechanical properties but fails due to the inclusion of the incorrect Statement 3.

Final Answer & Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: Option B

The Torque (T) equation of an induction motor is:

Torque = (3 ÷ ωs) × (s E22 R2) ÷ (R22 + (s X2)2)

Why Statement 3 is wrong:
Slip (s) = (Ns - Nr) ÷ Ns. At starting, the rotor speed (Nr) is 0. Therefore, s = Ns ÷ Ns = 1. Claiming it is zero is a fundamental error.

Why Statement 1 and 4 are correct:
While the position of the maximum torque shifts with R2, the actual peak value remains constant. This is why adding external resistance to slip-ring motors helps during start-up without reducing the peak capacity.

Q3. Nyquist Stability Criterion

Topic Introduction: The Nyquist Criterion is a graphical method in control theory that predicts closed-loop stability based on the open-loop frequency response. It is unique because it handles systems with "Right Half Plane" poles.

Focuses on the basic mechanism of mapping but omits the requirement of open-loop pole count.
The complete set of conditions required to determine Z (closed-loop poles).
Incorrectly assumes the criterion is limited only to minimum phase systems.

Final Answer & Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: Option B

The core of the Nyquist Criterion is the Principle of Argument, expressed as:

N = Z - P
  • N: Clockwise encirclements of the critical point (-1, 0).
  • P: Open-loop poles in the Right Half Plane.
  • Z: Resultant closed-loop poles in the Right Half Plane (must be 0 for stability).

Why Statement 3 is wrong:
Nyquist is specifically powerful because it does apply to non-minimum phase systems (systems with zeros in the right half plane) and systems that are unstable in open-loop.
